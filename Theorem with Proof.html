De Moivre's Theorem

STATEMENT:

For all real values of n, cos nθ + i sin nθ is a value of (cos θ + i sin θ)n.

Written more fully, the theorem states that “if n be integral, positive or negative, the value of (cos θ + i sin θ)n is cos nθ + i sin nθ ; if n be a fraction, positive or negative , one of the values of (cos θ + i sin θ)n is cos nθ + i sin nθ (the total number of values being equal to the denominator of n)”.

PROOF:

Case I: When n is positive integer.
By actual multiplication, we have

\begin{eqnarray*} (cos \theta_{1} + i sin \theta_{1})(cos \theta_{2} + i sin \theta_{2}) & = & cos \theta_{1}cos \theta_{2} - sin \theta_{1}sin \theta_{2} + i(sin \theta_{1}cos \theta_{2} + cos \theta_{1}sin \theta_{2})\\ & = & cos (\theta_{1} + \theta_{2}) + i sin (\theta_{1} + \theta_{2}). \end{eqnarray*} Similarly, \begin{eqnarray*} (cos \theta_{1} + i sin \theta_{1})(cos \theta_{2} + i sin \theta_{2})(cos \theta_{3} + i sin \theta_{3}) & = &\{ cos (\theta_{1} + \theta_{2}) + i sin (\theta_{1} + \theta_{2})\}(cos \theta_{3} + i sin \theta_{3})\\ & = & cos(\theta_{1} + \theta_{2} + \theta_{3}) + i sin(\theta_{1} + \theta_{2} + \theta_{3}) ,{\verb| as before|}. \end{eqnarray*} Proceeding in the way, the product of the n factors \begin{eqnarray*} (cos \theta_{1} + i sin \theta_{1})(cos \theta_{2} + i sin \theta_{2})\cdots (cos \theta_{n} + i sin \theta_{n}) & = & cos (\theta_{1} + \theta_{2} + \cdots + \theta_{n}) + i sin(\theta_{1} + \theta_{2} + \cdots + \theta_{n}). \end{eqnarray*} Put in this identity θ1 = θ2 = · · · = θn = θ. Then , we have $$ (cos \theta + i sin \theta)^{n} = cos n\theta + i sin n\theta .$$ Case II: When n is a negative integer. Let n=-m, then m is a positive integer, and \begin{eqnarray*} (cos \theta + i sin \theta)^{n} & = & (cos \theta + i sin \theta)^{-m}\\ & = & \frac{1}{(cos \theta + i sin \theta)^{m}}\\ & = & \frac{1}{(cos m\theta + i sin m\theta)} \verb| [by Case I]|\\ & = & \frac{cos m\theta - i sin m\theta}{(cos m\theta + i sin m \theta)(cos m\theta - i sin m \theta)}\\ & = & \frac{cos m\theta - i sin m\theta}{cos^{2} m\theta + sin^{2} m\theta}\\ & = & cos m\theta - i sin m\theta \\ & = & cos(-m) \theta + i sin (-m)\theta\\ & = & cos n\theta + i sin n\theta. \end{eqnarray*} Case III: When n is a fraction, positive or negative. $$ \verb| Let | n = \frac{p}{q}, \verb| where | q \verb| is a positive integer and | p \verb| is any integer , positive or negative.|$$ \begin{eqnarray*} \verb| Now , | (cos \frac{\theta}{q} + i sin \frac{\theta}{q})^{q} & = & cos (q \cdot \frac{\theta}{q}) + i sin (q \cdot \frac{\theta}{q}) \verb| , [by Case I]|\\ & = & cos \theta + i sin \theta . \end{eqnarray*} $$ \verb|Therefore , taking the | qth \verb| root of both sides, |\\ cos \frac{\theta}{q} + i sin \frac{\theta}{q} \verb| is one of the values of | (cos \theta + i sin \theta)^{\frac{1}{q}}.$$ Raise each of these quantities to the pth power. Then , one of the values of \begin{eqnarray*} (cos \theta + i sin \theta)^{\frac{p}{q}} & = & (cos \frac{\theta}{q} + i sin \frac{\theta}{q})^{p}, \\ i.e. ,& = & cos \frac{p\theta}{q} + i sin \frac{p\theta}{q} \verb| . [by Case I and Case II]| \end{eqnarray*} Hence , one of the values of (cos θ + i sin θ)n is cos nθ + i sin nθ. Thus , the theorem is established for all the rational values of n.

Note*. Even if n be irrational, cos nθ + i sin nθ is one of the values of (cos θ + i sin θ),
the total number of different values in this case being infinite.
